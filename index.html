Abstract

This article will briefly explain the long-lasting history of the Internal Combustion Engines (ICE). The ICE has been powering the world for centuries, really becoming big in 
the 1860’s (Starr, FS (2012) Development of the Poppet Type Exhaust Valve in the Internal Combustion Engine: Part I 1860–1930). I will discuss the basic operation of the ICEs 
and compare the types, showing how they differ in power, efficiency and impact on the environment. As the latter part of my title suggests I will go into detail over the 
present and future of the ICEs. As they are forced to become more efficient, as the need to combat climate change increases. This will be shown in the format of a table to 
clearly highlight the differences.

How Do They Work?

Internal combustions Engines (ICE) come in 2 main forms 2–stroke and 4-engines. They share parts within the engines, for example they both have a piston, crank case and cylinder 
etc. The way a 4-stroke operates is it starts with the induction strike; this is where a piston goes down the cylinder and draws in air mixed with fuel. Then follows the 
compression stroke. All valves are closed, and the piston rises causing ignition, after this the working stroke which raises the pressure and temperature. Finally, the exhaust 
stroke, which “expels” the gases. (Stone, RS (2012) Introduction to Internal Combustion Engines). The 2-stroke engine operates in a much shorter way. It includes the compression
stroke and the working stroke. The 4-stroke cycle is commonly referred to as “suck, squeeze, bang and blow”. (Stone, RS (2012) Introduction to Internal Combustion Engines) ICEs 
use these continued and controlled ignitions to create a force, which then powers the engines parts and makes everything work. Using pressure and gases in the process. 
(Zhu, HZ, et al (2023) Study on combustion and emission characteristics of a micro- rotary cam internal combustion engine).

Advancements and Efficiency improvements

As highlighted in my abstract the demand to make things more efficient is always increasing. Internal combustion engines are huge producers of greenhouse gases, due to them being powered by diesel and 
gas, which contribute to high consumption use. However, with the help of advancements Interanl combustion engines are aiming to continue to power our world. There are many different strategies proposed by engineers and
governments to make Internal combustion engines more environmentally friendly and efficient. I will be discussing two in this article, but this is in no way the limits to our future advancements. 
Low – temperature Combustion is one of the ways of improving thermal efficiency. It includes “improved mixture preparation fuel atomization, reduced combustion”. These in theory 
will help to decrease emissions but also improving thermal efficiency. Another interesting way of improving efficiency is using a “Laser Ignition System”. This system decreases 
combustion duration and heightens combustion efficiency, as the combustion doesn’t continue when it is no longer needed. (Dahham, RD (2022) Improving Thermal Efficiency of 
Internal Combustion Engines: Recent Progress and Remaining Challenges).

Environmental Impact and Challenges

99.8 % of global transport in 2020 was powered by ICEs (LEACH, FL et al (2020) The scope for improving the efficiency and environmental impact of internal combustion engines). 
This is how ICEs have a such a huge environmental impact and why there is so many challenges in improving them. As ICEs are used in almost forms of travel and are clearly 
fit for purpose the alternatives are far behind. The alternatives if rushed can have disastrous impacts on the environment and socially. The transport industry causes 25% of 
global CO2 emissions. Therefore, by default ICEs are a huge contributor to CO2 emissions. (LEACH, FL et al (2020) The scope for improving the efficiency and environmental 
impact of internal combustion engines).
