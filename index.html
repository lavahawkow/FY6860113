How Do They Work?

class="ex1"

Internal combustions Engines (ICE) come in 2 main forms 2–stroke and 4-engines. They share parts within the engines, for example they both have a piston, crank case and cylinder etc. The way a 4-stroke operates is it 
starts with the induction strike; this is where a piston goes down the cylinder and draws in air mixed with fuel. Then follows the compression stroke. All valves are closed, and the piston rises causing ignition, after 
this the working stroke which raises the pressure and temperature. Finally, the exhaust stroke, which “expels” the gases. (Stone, RS (2012) Introduction to Internal Combustion Engines). The 2-stroke engine operates in a 
much shorter way. It includes the compression stroke and the working stroke. The 4-stroke cycle is commonly referred to as “suck, squeeze, bang and blow”. (Stone, RS (2012) Introduction to Internal Combustion Engines) 
ICEs use these continued and controlled ignitions to create a force, which then powers the engines parts and makes everything work. Using pressure and gases in the process. (Zhu, HZ, et al (2023) Study on combustion and 
emission characteristics of a micro- rotary cam internal combustion engine).
